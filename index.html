Mu2e Straw‑Tube Tracker Engineering

Project Overview

The Mu2e tracker provides the primary momentum measurement for conversion electrons. The tracker must accurately and efficiently identify and measure 105 MeV/c electrons while rejecting backgrounds. The Mu2e tracker will be made of ~20,000 straw tubes, each of which is 5 mm in diameter. At the center, there is a gold-plated tungsten wire 25 microns in diameter that runs from end to end. The straws are filled with a gas mixture of Argon and carbon dioxide (80:20). When a charged particle, such as a potential signal electron, passes through a straw, it ionizes the gas, leaving a trail of free electrons and positive ions. A high voltage (~1500 V) is applied between the sense wire (anode) and the metalized straw wall (cathode). The electric field causes the free electrons from the ionization to drift rapidly toward the central wire. As they approach the wire, the electric field becomes strong enough that the electrons gain enough energy to cause further ionization in a process called an avalanche, creating a detectable electrical signal

What I Solved and How

    We encountered a few problems: how to fasten the straws at each end of the helical structure, how to keep the wire and mylar straw electrically separated so we can maintain a voltage across the straw, and how to manufacture precise components at scale and with repeatability for a run of 20,000 units. My solution is a modular fitting system with three nested components.

    The outer piece is a flat plate populated with hexagonal pylons that contain threaded holes (see left figure). Each pylon accepts a hollow, threaded cylindrical fitting, 5 millimeters in diameter. Inside that fitting sits a small cylindrical insert with a longitudinal slit. The plug board will be dipped in conductive paint and cured in an oven. We will solder the wire to a small 90 gram weight and slide that weight into the smallest fitting. The slit lets us insert the thin wire, it increases surface area for a custom tool to grip while screwing the insert into the plug board, and it allows the argon plus CO2 mixture to flood into the straws.

The mylar straws are extremely fragile, prone to crumpling or buckling under even slight pressure. Individually attaching more than 20,000 of them would be tedious and unscalable. To solve this, I designed a bulk-loading system that connects entire arrays of straws to the plug board at once. The setup features a pivoting infrastructure mounted on a linear rail and an apparatus that holds 64 straws in a precise lattice. A machined fixture secures the plug board in place beneath the assembly. By rotating a long lever arm, the lattice array travels smoothly down the rail toward the plug board, allowing the straws to slide onto their fittings in a single, controlled motion. This system minimizes handling, ensures alignment, and protects the delicate mylar during installation.

What I Learned

Throughout this process I honed my problem-solving skills. One of my favorite aspects of tackling industry-scale engineering challenges is identifying the core problem and applying physical first principles to develop a solution. I worked extensively in CAD, designing manufacturable components to industry standards, with careful attention to tolerances, modularity, and precise dimensions. Rapid prototyping played a central role, teaching me the value of iterative experimentation. Each prototype functioned like a controlled experiment: I varied certain elements while holding others constant to test solutions in practice, then implemented the successful designs in the final system.

The next step is to manufacture one full tracker plane and fill all straws with gas. Then, we can simulate a charged particle interaction with cosmic rays.
